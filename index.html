A Kindertransport Story

Life Disrupted

My great-grandmother Dory Knoll was nine years old when her world shattered.

Before November 1938, she lived in a third-floor apartment on Taborstraße in Vienna's second district—the Leopoldstadt, the heart of Jewish Vienna. Her father, Heinrich, was a watchmaker whose shop occupied the ground floor of their building. Her mother, Margarete, taught piano to neighborhood children in their sitting room, the notes floating down to the street below. On Friday evenings, the apartment filled with the smell of fresh challah and the glow of Shabbat candles.

Dory walked to school each morning past the Prater park, where the giant Ferris wheel turned slowly against the sky. She had a best friend named Ruth who lived two streets over. They would trade chocolates and whisper secrets during recess. Life was ordinary. Life was good.

Then came Kristallnacht.

Life Disrupted

On the night of November 9th, 1938, Dory woke to the sound of breaking glass. Her mother pulled her from bed and held her in the corner of the kitchen, away from the windows. Outside, she could hear shouting, the crack of wood splintering, her father's voice telling someone to stop, please stop.

The next morning, she saw the shop—every watch, every clock, every tiny gear scattered across the cobblestones. Her father's hands were bleeding. Within a week, Heinrich Knoll was taken to Dachau. Dory watched from the window as two men in uniforms led him to a truck. He looked up at her and smiled, but his eyes were terrified.

She never saw him again.

Kindertransport.

The Kindertransport

In the wake of Kristallnacht—the violent pogrom of November 9-10, 1938, in which Nazi forces destroyed a Jewish orphanage in Berlin along with countless other Jewish institutions—the British government agreed to accept unaccompanied Jewish children as refugees (United States Holocaust Memorial Museum). What followed was one of the largest organized rescue efforts of the Holocaust. Between December 1938 and May 1940, approximately 10,000 children, the vast majority of them Jewish, were brought from Nazi-controlled territories to Great Britain (United States Holocaust Memorial Museum).

The first transport arrived in Harwich on December 2, 1938, carrying 200 children from that very Berlin orphanage that had been destroyed just weeks earlier (United States Holocaust Memorial Museum). The rescue operation required an intricate network of organizations working across borders. Jewish organizations within the German Reich planned the transports, including the Reich Representation of Jews in Germany and the Jewish Community Organization, known as the Kultusgemeinde, in Vienna (United States Holocaust Memorial Museum). These groups prioritized children whose emigration was most urgent—those whose parents were already in concentration camps, those who could no longer be supported, and orphans (United States Holocaust Memorial Museum).

Dory Knoll fit all three categories.

The Goodbye

The train station was chaos. Hundreds of children pressed against each other on the platform, their breath rising in white clouds in the January cold. Each child wore a number on a tag around their neck. Dory's was 342.

Her mother knelt before her, straightening her coat for the hundredth time. Margarete's face was pale, her eyes rimmed red, but she was not crying. She had promised herself she would not cry.

"You will be brave," her mother said. It was not a question.

Dory nodded. She clutched her small suitcase—one suitcase, that was all they were allowed. Inside: two dresses, her nightgown, a photograph of her parents on their wedding day, and a small porcelain dog that had sat on her windowsill since she was a baby.

"When the war is over, I will come for you," her mother continued. "Or you will come home to me. Either way, we will be together again. Do you understand?"

"Yes, Mutti."

The Goodbye

Her mother pressed something into her hand—a folded piece of paper. "My letters. I will write to you every week. And you will write to me. And the time will pass quickly, you'll see."

A whistle blew. The children began to surge toward the train.

Margarete pulled Dory into an embrace so tight it hurt. Dory could feel her mother's heart beating against her own, fast and desperate.

"I love you," her mother whispered into her hair. "I love you more than the whole world."

Then she let go.

Dory was swept forward by the crowd, her small body carried by the tide of children moving toward the train. She found a window seat and pressed her face to the glass, searching the platform. There—her mother, standing perfectly still while the crowd churned around her. Their eyes met.

The train lurched forward.

Dory watched her mother's figure grow smaller and smaller until she was just a speck, and then nothing at all.

An Impossible Choice

For the parents left behind, the decision to send their children away was almost unimaginable. They faced an impossible choice: keep their children close under a regime that branded them as outcasts and enemies, or send them alone to a foreign country, not knowing for how long or who would care for them (Yad Vashem). Many parents kissed their children goodbye at train stations and harbors across Central Europe—Berlin, Vienna, Prague—knowing in their hearts they might never see them again. Almost none of them ever did (Yad Vashem).

The children were permitted to bring only limited belongings: one small suitcase, no valuables, and no more than 10 Reichsmarks (United States Holocaust Memorial Museum). The artifacts that survive tell the story of what mattered most in those final moments—the United States Holocaust Memorial Museum's collection includes a teddy bear carried by Jack Hellman when he left Germany in early 1939; Yad Vashem holds a toy puppy brought by fourteen-year-old Marion Rochmann from Berlin (United States Holocaust Memorial Museum; Yad Vashem).

However, rescue came with a price: private citizens or organizations had to guarantee payment of £50 for each child's care, education, and eventual emigration—a sum that was supposed to ensure the children would not become a burden on the British state (United States Holocaust Memorial Museum).

The Journey

The journey took two days.

The train car was packed with children—some crying, some silent, some trying to comfort the younger ones. A girl about Dory's age sat beside her, clutching a teddy bear with matted fur. Her name was Hannah, and she was from Berlin. They held hands without speaking.

At the German-Dutch border, Nazi officers boarded the train. Dory watched one of them walk down the aisle, examining papers, peering into faces. When he reached her row, he stopped. He looked at her number tag. Then at her face.

"Jüdisch?" he asked.

"Ja," she whispered.

He moved on.

When the train crossed into the Netherlands, something changed. The tension in the car seemed to ease, just slightly. A woman from the Dutch Red Cross boarded and handed out chocolate and sandwiches. It was the first food Dory had eaten in almost a day, but she could barely swallow. Her throat felt like it had been packed with stones.

That night, she slept sitting up, her head against the cold window, dreaming of her mother's hands smoothing her hair.

Arrival in England

The ship docked at Harwich on a gray morning. Dory walked down the gangplank with the other children, her legs unsteady after the crossing. Everything looked different here—the buildings, the signs she couldn't read, the people speaking in a language she didn't understand.

Because no one had sponsored her, Dory was taken to Dovercourt Bay. The cabins had no heat. The children slept under piles of blankets, their breath visible in the dark.

Every few days, adults would come to the camp—potential foster families, looking for children to take home. The children would line up, and the adults would walk past them, examining them like items at a market. Dory learned to stand up straight, to smile, to look healthy and capable.

Three weeks later, she was chosen.

The Hartleys were a middle-aged couple from Leeds. They had no children of their own. Mrs. Hartley was a nurse; Mr. Hartley worked at a textile factory. They were kind, patient people who spoke slowly and pointed at objects to help Dory learn English.

Apple. Chair. Window. Home.

It took months before she could hold a real conversation. It took years before England felt like anything other than exile.

Dovercourt Bay

Upon arrival, children with sponsors traveled to London to meet their foster families. Those without sponsors faced a different reality: they were housed at Dovercourt Bay, a summer camp established by Billy Butlin that had only enjoyed one season before closing. It reopened in what would become the coldest winter on record to shelter the refugee children (The Wiener Holocaust Library).

A report from the Women's Voluntary Service described the camp starkly: "a clearing station for German and Austrian Jewish and non-Aryan refugee children until arrangements can be made for their future. These children are from five to 18 years of age, and their parents are mostly dead or in concentration camps" (The Wiener Holocaust Library).

Most transports departed by train from major cities, with children from smaller towns and villages traveling to these collection points to join the convoys. The trains crossed through the Netherlands before the children boarded ships to Harwich (United States Holocaust Memorial Museum). The transports continued until war made them impossible. The last transport from Germany departed on September 1, 1939—the very day World War II began. The last transport from the Netherlands left on May 14, 1940, the same day the Dutch army surrendered to German forces (United States Holocaust Memorial Museum).

Learning the Truth

The letter arrived on a Tuesday.

Dory was seventeen years old. She had lived with the Hartleys for seven years. She spoke English fluently now, with only the faintest trace of an accent. She had finished school, made friends, learned to bake Yorkshire pudding. She called Mrs. Hartley "Mum."

But she had never stopped writing letters to her mother—letters she had no address to send to, letters that piled up in a drawer in her room like prayers no one could answer.

The Red Cross letter was thin. Just one page.

She read it standing in the kitchen. Mrs. Hartley watched her face change.

Heinrich Knoll. Died Dachau, March 1939.

He had been dead for months before she even arrived in England. All those nights she had imagined him coming to get her, walking through the door of the Hartley house with his watchmaker's hands and his gentle smile—he had already been gone.

Margarete Knoll. Deported to Auschwitz, 1944. No record of survival.

Her mother. Her mother who had promised to come for her. Her mother who had said either way, we will be together again.

Dory did not cry. Not then. She folded the letter carefully, placed it in the wooden box where she kept her mother's last note, and walked outside into the gray English afternoon.

No Happy Endings

The children who survived carried wounds that would last a lifetime. They faced emotional distress, intense loneliness, and a profound sense of detachment from the world around them. The full extent of their loss—what had happened to their parents, their siblings, their homes—was only revealed in 1945 (Yad Vashem).

The vast majority of the parents who had made the agonizing decision to send their children to safety were murdered in the Holocaust (United States Holocaust Memorial Museum). The Kinder, as they came to be called, survived the war, but as Yad Vashem notes, they did not get happy endings (Yad Vashem). Many changed their names over time, sometimes dramatically—a transformation that illustrates how deeply the experience reshaped their very identities (The Wiener Holocaust Library).

For decades, many survivors kept their stories private. It was not until 1988 that Bertha Leverton organized a reunion of former Kindertransportees, an event that brought renewed public attention to this chapter of history and led to the formation of the Kindertransport Association the following year (The Wiener Holocaust Library).
